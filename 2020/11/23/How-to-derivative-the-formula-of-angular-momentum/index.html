How to derivative the formula of angular momentum

Posted on 2020-11-23

This time I am going to show how to get the formula of the angular momentum!

Process!!

As angular momentum, we can consider that the object is doing a circular motion around a particular point, just as the graph shows

Well is still the graph I used last time. In this circle, we consider radius as r, direction, which can also be said as the tangent line, we consider it as p, and I add an extra quantity which is the arc that the object moves, I record it as Δl.

According to the formula to calculate the area of the sector, we can easily calculate the area of the sector with length Δl is 1/2rΔl, than, we let both side of the equation divided by the difference of time Δt, so we can get Area/Δt=1/2rΔl/Δt, in this formula, Δl/Δt , which is the linear velocity, and that means the velocity of the object at a particular point on its orbital, can be recorded as V , so all after all, the formula is S(Area)/Δt=1/2r·V

In this strange formula, we know that 1/2r·v is constant,so rv is constant, so r·v·m which is the product of radius, linear velocity and mass, according tp that they are all constants, so they are the conservation terms. In addition, as we have learnt before, the momentum equals to mv , so the formula can be transferred into rt(I like record the momentum as t), so the r, radius and the momentum t, are the conserve terms.

Well that is quite amazing from calculating the area of the sector to get to know the conservation terms are radius and momentum. And hence we can finally get to know the angular momentum equals to rt·sinα and αin this formula is the angle that the object moves. In this formula, due to that the angular momentum is always conserved, hence the increase of any of the r and v, will effect the other term, which means, if the radius of the circular orbital increases, the velocity will be decreased and vice versa.

Furthermore, base on the formula we got above, if I let both side of the formula divided by t, hence the angular momentum side turns to the rate of change of the angular momentum and the right side of the equation turns to the rmv/t and v/t is acceleration, hence the equation turns to rma, because of F =ma, hence the equation is rF, which is same as the equation for lever!, Which is the force multiply moment. Hence all in all, we can get another principle, which is that the moment is the rate of change of the angular momentum.
