Wankel Engine

Posted on 2020-07-26

Normal Engine

Nowadays, autos are more commonly separated, hence the using of engine is more of great importance, and now let’s see several pictures about normal engines.

Here is the engine of Audi. As the core of the whole car, engine or motor is a machine designed to convert one form of energy into mechanical energy. Heat engines, like the internal combustion engine, burn a fuel to create heat which is then used to do work. Electric motors convert electrical energy into mechanical motion, pneumatic motors use compressed air, and clockwork motors in wind-up toys use elastic energy. In biological systems, molecular motors, like myosins in muscles, use chemical energy to create forces and ultimately motion.

Just like we have learned in Grade 9, the engine works with 4 stages, it uses the force which is created by burning fuel to push the spiale to move the whole car.

And the most common engine is Combustion Engine or Internal Combustion Engine, those two types of engine works as I mentioned, there’s the combustion of the fuel in the engine with an oxidizer in a chamber. During the reaction, high pressure and high temperature is created and they apply force to push the engine.

Wankel Engine

Well, after talking about normal engine, it is term to introduce a particular engine which is called Wankel Engine.

Here I show the picture of the Wankel Engine. The difference between normal engines and Wankel Engine is obvious. We ca see that the central part of the Wankel engine is a triangle shaped rotor, and the gear structure is added with a axle in the center. Similar to normal heated engine, Wankel engine is an internal combustion engine as well which uses eccentric rotary design to convert pressure into rotating motion.

The Wankel engine was invented in the year of 1957 by a German engineer Felix Wankel. All parts of a rotary engine rotate in one direction. This is opposed to the common reciprocating piston engine, which has pistons instantly and rapidly changing direction 180 degrees. In contrast to the reciprocating piston designs, the Wankel engine delivers advantages of simplicity, smoothness, compactness, high revolutions per minute, and a high power-to-weight ratio, so in contrast, the Wankel engine can do more work because it abandoned useless straight-line action, which will waste plenty of energy and efficiency. Moreover, the engines with same power, the Wankel engine is much smaller and lighter.

The reason why I praised it as an legend is that different from other engines. When Wankel engine rotates for one circle, the engine will ignite for three times and do work for three times! Two times more than others!

The core of the Wankel engine is the rotor, which creates the turning motion. It is similar in shape to a Reuleaux triangle, with the sides being somewhat flatter. Wankel engines deliver three power pulses per revolution of the rotor using the Otto cycle. However, the output shaft turns three times faster giving one power pulse per revolution at its output. The result is that the rotor turns much slower than the output shaft at one third of its speed because of its toothed gearing to the rotor. In one revolution, the rotor gives power pulses and exhausting simultaneously, while the four stages of the Otto cycle occur at separate times. For comparison, in a two-stroke piston engine there is one power pulse per crankshaft revolution (the same as a Wankel engine’s output shaft) with one in two revolutions in a four-stroke piston engine.
